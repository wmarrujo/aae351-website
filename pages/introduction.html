Introduction

Background

In the US, unexploded ordnance poses a public health risk at 16,000 domestic inactive military ranges, with individual ranges covering up to 500 square miles. The unexploded ordnance goes back to as far as the Civil War, with degradation over the years increasing its sensitivity to disturbance. The EPA estimates that unexploded ordnance in the US would require the largest environmental cleanup ever, costing at least $14 billion. With ordnance disposal there is an inherent danger a 1,100 lb bomb from WWII buried 23 ft below the ground in Germany detonated while Explosive Ordnance Disposal professionals were evacuating the area and preparing to disarm it, killing three and injuring six.

Motivation

An effective ordinance cleanup solution is necessary in the United States. Having 500 square miles of potentially hazardous ranges presents a real danger to the people operating in and living beside the ranges. There are many stories over the years of people being accidentally injured or killed by these supposedly inert weapons and in way protecting the citizens of the nation, it is the government’s responsibility to remove that threat. For reference, the EPA has a running incident report to see the extent to which this is a problem that must be solved.

Mission

Our team’s mission is to provide a solution to reclaim areas in the US that have been plagued with unexploded ordnance. Above all else we will prioritize human lives and aim to create a safe work environment that will minimal risk while still having the desired impact.

Schedule

According to an analysis done by the RAND organization, there were at least 196 civilian casualties involving unexploded ordnance since WWII. This averages to about 2.5 casualties per year. As such, a timeframe of clearing the ordinance from US Soil of 10 years would still allow for up to 25 casualties. Seeing as the area needed to be cleared is 500 square miles, a time frame on the order of a decade is probably the only reasonable schedule.

Assumptions

  • The ordnance in question are military grade and include significant metal components.
  • The cleanup effort is a priority for the ranges in question.
  • The land reclaimed will be for public use.
  • Any existing technology and training required can be acquired through the government.
  • The cleanup effort will be heavily monitored if not done directly by the government - most likely the environmental protection agency.
  • Bomb technicians are paid engineering wages.
  • Terrain is accessible by truck.
  • The cleanup effort for any given area will be on the order of years to decades.
